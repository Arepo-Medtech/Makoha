# P1.3 — Business Model and Moat Thesis

**Status:** DONE — MIXED-MODEL VALIDATION OPEN  
**Date:** 21 July 2026  
**Decision owner:** Founder  
**Decision record:** M-D017, superseding M-D015  
**Supports:** M-D005 regulated pharmacist consultation-assurance wedge; M-D011 Pharmacy 777 entry strategy

## Objective

Select who pays, how Makoha charges and what can become defensible; compare credible revenue architectures; show provisional unit economics; and identify the evidence that could validate or kill the model.

## Decision — amended by founder direction

Makoha will pursue a **non-exclusive mixed enterprise-and-consult model** for regulated pharmacist-led care:

1. A pharmacy group, network or other accountable enterprise pays a base governance/platform fee plus site and, where useful, clinician/user bands.
2. Patients may pay a transparent per-consult service/access fee for a Makoha-enabled pharmacist-led pathway.
3. The patient fee contributes to the shared cost of doctor availability, clinical escalation/continuity and the technology pathway; an actual doctor consultation may be included or separately priced under the disclosed service model.
4. Pharmacy, group, employer, insurer, health-system or other payer service agreements may subsidise or fully offset the patient fee and fund doctor capacity commitments.
5. Implementation, integration, governed evidence projects and later platform licensing remain separate revenue arms.
6. The pharmacist remains accountable within the pharmacist scope. Out-of-scope or clinically unsuitable presentations are routed to an available doctor or an appropriate urgent/emergency pathway without commercial influence or delay.
7. Makoha will not use prescription revenue share, prescription-contingent charges, outcome-linked fees or automatic conversion from the approved zero-fee discovery phase.

The mixed structure distributes cost across the enterprise receiving governance value, the patient receiving convenient access and the service partners funding access. It can lower the amount any pharmacy must absorb while supporting the doctor capacity required by a deliberately narrow pharmacist scope.

## Revenue architectures considered

| Architecture | Buyer and charge | Advantages | Material weaknesses | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Per-clinician SaaS | Pharmacist, independent site or group pays per authorised user/month | Simple, familiar and supports independent/small-site access | Frames Makoha as a scribe if unbundled; weak fit where users rotate; downward price pressure from documentation tools | SELECTED as an optional tier/band, not the foundation |
| B2B per-consult usage | Group/site/payer funds activity under a service agreement | Variable cost follows utilisation; can subsidise patients and doctor capacity | Can penalise adoption if it is the only pharmacy charge; unpredictable without a minimum commitment | SELECTED as an optional service-agreement component |
| Enterprise assurance licence | Group pays base annual fee plus site bands; implementation/evidence work charged separately | Aligns with central governance value, multi-site assurance and recurring QMS obligations | Longer procurement cycle; does not by itself fund doctor availability or every clinical episode | SELECTED as foundation |
| Patient per-consult fee | Patient pays a disclosed fee for the Makoha-enabled service pathway; may be subsidised under service agreements | Adds a utilisation-linked revenue arm; helps fund doctor availability and can reduce pharmacy licence burden | Affordability, informed financial consent, refund/waiver, provider/biller and clinical-incentive risks | SELECTED subject to legal, clinical and consumer-design gates |

Individual users and consultations will also be metered for access control, safety monitoring and capacity planning. Clinical routing, prescribing and escalation cannot depend on whether a fee is collected.

### Consultation revenue and doctor-capacity test

No per-consult price is approved. The operating model must be tested using:

`Net consultation contribution = (chargeable consultations × disclosed patient fee) + service-agreement subsidy − payment costs − pharmacy/provider share − doctor-capacity cost − variable technology/support cost − waivers/refunds`

`Doctor-capacity coverage ratio = (consultation revenue allocated to doctor capacity + contracted service-agreement funding) ÷ doctor standby and delivered-consult cost`

The model should not launch unless the doctor-capacity coverage ratio is at least 1.0 under the agreed service level, including reasonable out-of-scope and peak-demand sensitivity. A patient fee alone must not be assumed sufficient: enterprise or payer agreements may provide a minimum capacity payment, while the patient component reduces the net cost to pharmacies and varies with use.

The commercial design must decide whether an actual doctor consultation is included in the original fee, requires a disclosed top-up, or is paid entirely through a service agreement. That decision depends on the provider-of-record, doctor network, jurisdiction, Medicare/private-billing position and pharmacy agreement.

### Minimum economic conditions

The model passes only if:

1. the first-year contract covers all incremental implementation and support effort;
2. recurring revenue carries an evidenced path to at least 60% gross margin after stabilisation;
3. integration does not require a bespoke rebuild for each site;
4. the buyer values assurance beyond functions already supplied by its clinical or dispensing system; and
5. several non-exclusive customers, or a scalable platform channel, can share the fixed regulatory and QMS burden;
6. doctor availability and out-of-scope continuity are contractually funded at the required service level; and
7. patient pricing remains accessible through pharmacy/payer subsidy, waiver or hardship arrangements.

The early enterprise should not be expected to recover all platform regulatory investment from one design partner. A durable case will likely require **[ASSUMPTION — LOW CONFIDENCE]** three to eight comparable enterprise groups or an equivalent platform-licensing channel.

## Market stress test

### Qiri

Qiri is the closest public direct comparator: it presents a pharmacy-wide clinical reasoning and dispensing platform, integrates with pharmacy systems, uses per-site custom pricing and promotes audit trails and cross-network learning. Its published value claims are vendor claims, not independently verified. Makoha should not initially compete across dispensing, script processing or general pharmacy intelligence. It must prove a narrower, regulated expanded-scope **consultation-assurance** function that is complementary to the pharmacy's existing systems. Sources: [Qiri product](https://qiri.ai/au/product), [Qiri software terms](https://qiri.ai/au/software-user-terms-and-agreement).

### Heidi and Lyrebird

Heidi and Lyrebird make documentation, templates and clinical administration increasingly available at low per-user or practice-wide subscription prices. Lyrebird publicly lists Australian practice plans from $299 + GST/month, scaling to $1,649 + GST/month, and Heidi offers free and paid documentation plans. Makoha cannot defend premium pricing through note generation. Its fee must be supported by regulated workflow assurance, evidence, safety controls, integration and cross-site governance. Sources: [Lyrebird Australian pricing](https://www.lyrebirdhealth.com/au/lyrebird-pricing), [Heidi pricing](https://www.heidihealth.com/en-us/pricing).

### Eucalyptus

Eucalyptus is an end-to-end consumer digital-health operator spanning patient acquisition, telehealth, pharmacy fulfilment and ongoing care. It is a vertical care-delivery benchmark rather than a direct pharmacy-SaaS comparator. Makoha should remain B2B assurance infrastructure in the entry phase rather than fund direct-to-consumer care delivery and acquisition. Source: [Eucalyptus](https://www.eucalyptus.health/).

## Moat thesis

Makoha's moat is **not** an AI model or ownership of patient data. It is the governed combination of:

1. permissioned, structured longitudinal consultation-assurance events;
2. traceable eligibility, exclusion, red-flag, source, decision, override, escalation, documentation and follow-up evidence;
3. a maintained safety case, clinical evidence and Class III-capable QMS;
4. workflow and clinical-system integrations that preserve provenance and accountability; and
5. cross-site performance learning using only contractually permitted, de-identified or aggregated data.

The detailed thesis and data-rights limits are in `03_ARTEFACTS/P1.3_MOAT_MEMO.md`.

## Falsifiers and kill criteria

Revise or stop the commercial thesis if any of the following is evidenced:

- Existing clinical systems or Qiri provide the incremental assurance outcome without Makoha.
- Buyers will pay less than the direct cost of delivery, compliance and support.
- Bespoke implementation consumes more than first-year contract value.
- Required manual clinical oversight prevents a credible path above 50% gross margin.
- Group procurement cannot coexist with site-owner, data-controller or responsible-pharmacist authority.
- Makoha cannot secure lawful rights to use derived, de-identified aggregate performance evidence across deployments.
- The product cannot demonstrate a measurable safety, completeness, governance or efficiency improvement without unsafe clinical reliance.
- Patient charges create material access inequity, surprise billing or unsafe avoidance/delay of care.
- Doctor capacity cannot be contracted and funded without making clinically inappropriate referral volume economically attractive.

## Validation plan

During P1.2 interviews and Pharmacy 777 discovery:

1. Test group, site, patient and third-party-payer willingness and budget ownership separately.
2. Ask for an actual budget route, prior comparable purchase and procurement threshold—not an abstract willingness-to-pay score.
3. Present the mixed enterprise, site, patient-consult and service-agreement structure without presenting provisional ranges as approved prices.
4. Capture current system/vendor costs and the incremental function Makoha must outperform.
5. Measure deployment/support effort and build a bottom-up COGS model before an external paid offer.
6. Require explicit data-rights discussion: controller roles, permitted derived metrics, model training, deletion, portability and cross-site aggregation.
7. Map the complete out-of-scope pathway: clinical threshold, doctor availability, response time, provider of record, handover, fee disclosure, patient choice, alternative pathways and emergency escalation.
8. Test patient fee sensitivity, subsidies, waivers, refunds and willingness to proceed before approving any price.
9. Obtain advice on the billing entity, consumer law, GST, Medicare/private billing, professional indemnity, fee splitting and service-agreement structure.
