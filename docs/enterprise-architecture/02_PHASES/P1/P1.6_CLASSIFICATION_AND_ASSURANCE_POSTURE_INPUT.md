# P1.6 Input — Classification and Enterprise-Assurance Posture

**Date:** 20 July 2026

**Decision:** M-D007

## Founder direction

Makoha will not minimise its regulatory posture merely to preserve an MVP pathway. A false-reassurance or “safe not to progress” triage outcome can delay urgent treatment and may contribute to death or severe deterioration. The shared platform will therefore be engineered to a **Class III-capable enterprise assurance target** while the legally correct Australian classification is determined.

This is a quality-system uplift program, not a reason to suspend all work. Synthetic engineering, quality-system establishment, risk management, verification, clinical-evidence planning and counsel engagement can proceed in parallel. Supply and patient-facing use remain gated.

## Regulatory distinction

Makoha cannot elect a legal class by preference alone. Under the TGA framework, classification follows the manufacturer's intended purpose, the functions performed, the user receiving the output and the highest applicable classification rule.

The current rules create three plausible product-boundary outcomes:

| Product boundary | Provisional classification hypothesis | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Direct patient/user triage result or intervention recommendation where error/absence could cause death or severe deterioration | **Class III candidate** | Rules 4.5(1) and 4.7(1) place direct diagnosis/screening or treatment/intervention specification at Class III for the highest-consequence conditions. |
| Information or treatment recommendation only to a relevant health professional, who makes the decision | **Class IIb candidate** | Rules 4.5(2) and 4.7(2) generally classify the corresponding high-consequence professional-support function one class lower. |
| Pure record transfer, workflow or administrative documentation without therapeutic analysis | **Potential non-device/separate lower-risk function** | Depends on intended purpose and whether the function analyses information for a medical purpose. |

Sources: [TGA active-device classification guidance](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/classifying-active-medical-devices-australia-including-software-based-medical-devices) and [TGA software regulation overview](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/understanding-how-we-regulate-software-based-medical-devices).

`[REGULATORY HYPOTHESIS — NOT A DETERMINATION]` Makoha's direct triage function may attract Class III if it provides the result or intervention direction itself across potentially fatal or rapidly deteriorating presentations. The pharmacist-facing consultation-assurance function may instead fall within Class IIb if it only informs a relevant health professional. Qualified classification advice must apply the exact intended-purpose statement and architecture.

## Architecture recommendation

Use one enterprise quality system but preserve separable regulated product boundaries:

1. **Makoha Triage** — direct safety triage and disposition; provisional Class III candidate.
2. **Makoha Professional Assurance** — pharmacist/clinician-facing information and recommendations; provisional Class IIb candidate.
3. **Makoha Record and Workflow Services** — administrative, provenance and longitudinal-record functions assessed separately.

This avoids weakening the safety system while also avoiding an unnecessary claim that every shared administrative function is itself Class III. Where functions cannot be genuinely separated, the highest applicable classification governs the combined device.

## Enterprise QMS uplift

The target control baseline is:

- ISO 13485-aligned medical-device quality management system.
- ISO 14971-aligned risk management with explicit under-triage, false reassurance and automation-bias hazards.
- IEC 62304 **Class C engineering target** for software whose failure could contribute to death or serious injury; final applicability to be confirmed.
- IEC 62366-1-aligned usability and human-factors engineering.
- Clinical evaluation plan aligned to TGA SaMD clinical-evidence guidance.
- Cybersecurity, secure-development, vulnerability and version/change controls appropriate to connected medical-device software.
- Supplier, model, dataset, terminology, pharmacology and clinical-content qualification.
- Design history, requirements traceability, verification/validation, release authority and configuration control.
- CAPA, complaints, vigilance, post-market surveillance and field-action readiness.
- Independent clinical-safety governance and documented residual-risk acceptance.

These standards are an engineering/QMS target pending a formal standards-applicability assessment; they are not asserted here as a complete legal checklist.

## Existing assets that transfer

Makoha is not starting from zero. The repository already documents useful design-control evidence:

- fail-closed verification;
- human-sign-off gates;
- immutable/hash-chained evidence and WORM-backed records;
- clinically attested MIRAGE evaluation;
- dose firewalls and signed pharmacology knowledge;
- mock/live separation;
- contract tests and blocking CI gates; and
- provenance and receipt chains.

These are strong technical controls, but they do not yet constitute a complete certified QMS, clinical evaluation or conformity-assessment package.

## Immediate workstreams

1. **Intended purpose and product boundary:** freeze exact users, outputs, clinical claims, indications, contraindications and whether triage is direct or professional-mediated.
2. **Classification memorandum:** map every function to Rules 4.5–4.8 and identify the highest applicable class per separable product.
3. **QMS gap assessment:** map current repository evidence to design controls, risk management, software lifecycle, clinical evidence and post-market obligations.
4. **Safety case:** create the top-level hazard analysis for under-triage, false reassurance, delayed escalation, dose error, unavailable evidence, identity mismatch and clinician automation bias.
5. **Clinical evidence strategy:** define analytical validation, clinical performance, human-factors validation and prospective evidence needs. TGA notes that real-world evidence can complement but does not replace technical/analytical validation. [TGA real-world evidence guidance](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/medical-devices/application-and-market-authorisation/supply-medical-device/real-world-evidence)
6. **Regulatory program:** determine manufacturer/sponsor route, conformity-assessment strategy, ARTG pathway and pre-submission engagement.

## Gate interpretation

- **Enterprise uplift:** authorised to proceed now.
- **Synthetic development and verification:** authorised within existing governance.
- **Real-data research:** privacy, ethics and regulatory pathway dependent.
- **Clinical supply or patient-facing use:** blocked until the applicable conformity/ARTG or other lawful pathway is established.

This distinction keeps regulatory work from becoming organisational paralysis while preserving the release gate.
