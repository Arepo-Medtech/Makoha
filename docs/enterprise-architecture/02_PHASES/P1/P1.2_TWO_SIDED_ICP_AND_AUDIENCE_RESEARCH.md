## Objective

Define the first economic buyer, clinical user and patient beneficiary; state their jobs-to-be-done, buying triggers, objections and disqualifiers; and convert the result into a falsifiable design-partner evidence plan.

It does **not** establish willingness to pay for Makoha. That remains a field-validation obligation.

## Market-role map

| Role | First definition | What success means |
|---|---|---|
| Economic buyer | Owner, CEO, operations lead or clinical-governance lead of a regional multi-site community pharmacy group delivering or preparing for expanded-scope services | Consistent, auditable service delivery across sites without unsustainable governance or administration load |
| Primary user | Appropriately trained and authorised pharmacist prescriber, including a group clinical lead | A complete, protocol-aligned consultation record that supports—but never replaces—the pharmacist's clinical decision |
| Patient beneficiary | Adult eligible for a defined pharmacy service who values timely, local care | Safe, private and understandable care with informed consent, transparent cost, choice, escalation and continuity |
| Influencers | Referring GPs, programme administrators, indemnity providers, regulators, clinical-system vendors and professional bodies | Confidence that scope, accountability, communication and evidence are preserved |

Makoha therefore has a three-role adoption problem, not a single persona: the buyer carries organisational risk, the pharmacist retains professional accountability, and the patient bears clinical consequences.

## Primary ideal-customer profile

**Beachhead ICP:** an Australian multi-site pharmacy group that is already delivering or preparing to deliver expanded-scope pharmacist services; has central operational or clinical-governance ownership; can authorise a deliberately small pilot subset; uses an approved clinical information system; and is willing to evaluate an additional assurance layer using synthetic, retrospective de-identified or otherwise lawfully approved data.

**Preferred candidate and sequence (M-D011):** Pharmacy 777, contracting through its WA/Perth leadership structure, with the Toowoomba, Queensland prescribing-service workflow as the first proposed evidence setting and controlled expansion into WA after applicable gates. Every arrangement must be non-exclusive. The candidate choice remains provisional until authority, system fit and appetite are confirmed.

**Why this segment first:**

- Multiple sites make inconsistency, supervision and audit evidence material.
- A central buyer can approve a group-level design partnership.
- The buyer has more ability than a single-site operator to fund implementation and integration.
- The segment is smaller and more accessible than a national banner, while still producing cross-site evidence.
- Regional access aligns with the public policy purpose of expanded-scope pharmacy services.

### Segment priority

| Segment | Priority | Reason |
|---|---:|---|
| Pharmacy 777, beginning with group leadership and the Toowoomba workflow | 1 | WA alignment, current QLD prescribing service, digital-health adoption and a credible path to WA expansion |
| Other regional multi-site groups preparing for or operating expanded scope | 2 | Essential comparator evidence and fallback; prevents single-account bias |
| Single-site independents with an authorised prescriber | 3 | Useful workflow evidence, but lower budget and limited cross-site governance pain |
| National banners and very large groups | 4 | High value but slower procurement, integration, policy and build-versus-buy decisions |
| Direct-to-patient triage | Excluded from entry wedge | Highest clinical and regulatory consequence; no patient-facing release is authorised |
| General pharmacy dispensing workflow | Excluded | Weak fit with the selected consultation-assurance wedge |

### Qualification signals

A strong candidate has at least four of the following:

1. Two or more trained/authorised pharmacist prescribers or a near-term training plan.
2. More than one service location.
3. A named clinical-governance or service-operations owner.
4. Active concerns about documentation quality, exclusions, escalation, referrals or audit readiness.
5. Capacity to run a governed evidence protocol.
6. A clinical-system integration pathway and permission to evaluate it.
7. Willingness to provide time, baseline data and written success criteria.

### Anti-ICP and disqualifiers

- Seeks autonomous diagnosis, prescribing or removal of pharmacist judgement.
- Wants patient-facing deployment before regulatory, clinical and release gates close.
- Cannot provide private consultations, consent or approved documentation processes.
- Will not support incident reporting, escalation, audit or evidence collection.
- Requires unapproved use of identifiable patient data.
- Has no accountable buyer, no authorised users or no budget/process for evaluation.

## Jobs to be done

### Economic buyer

> When we expand pharmacist prescribing across sites, I need evidence that each consultation followed the approved pathway and documentation standard, so we can improve access and grow the service without multiplying clinical, professional and reputational risk.

Functional outcomes: standardisation, record completeness, same-day finalisation, defensible escalation, quality indicators and lower governance burden.

Emotional outcome: confidence that growth is controlled rather than fragile.

### Pharmacist user

> When I assess an expanded-scope presentation, I need a protocol-aligned assurance workflow that helps me capture the right history, identify exclusions and red flags, communicate or escalate appropriately, and complete the record, so I can make and defend my own clinical decision efficiently.

Functional outcomes: fewer missed fields, clearer exclusions, easier referral and complete documentation.

Professional outcome: preserve autonomy and accountability; Makoha must not imply that an algorithm made the decision.

### Patient beneficiary

> When I need timely care for an eligible condition, I want a private and convenient pharmacy consultation that explains cost, choices, treatment and escalation clearly, so I can access care without sacrificing safety, dignity, continuity or informed choice.

## Buying and adoption triggers

1. Joining a new state expanded-scope programme or adding conditions.
2. Onboarding newly trained pharmacist prescribers.
3. Scaling from one store to several stores.
4. An internal audit, incident, complaint, insurer query or incomplete record.
5. Inconsistent eligibility, exclusions, same-day finalisation or GP communication.
6. A clinical-system procurement or integration change.
7. Leadership concern that service revenue is constrained by pharmacist time or governance overhead.

## Objections and required answers

| Likely objection | Required evidence or product response |
|---|---|
| “Our mandated clinical system already does this.” | Demonstrate one complementary, measurable assurance function without duplicate entry; otherwise stop. |
| “AI may deskill or overrule the pharmacist.” | Human-authored decision, visible sources/logic, uncertainty and escalation; training and competency monitoring; no autonomous output. |
| “The pharmacist remains liable.” | Agree explicitly; show that Makoha improves evidence and consistency without transferring or obscuring accountability. |
| “This creates privacy and consent risk.” | Data minimisation, approved environments, explicit consent where required, access control, audit and no uncontrolled model training. |
| “Is it regulator-approved?” | Use the regulated SaMD planning posture; make no approval claim; restrict any study to counsel-approved evidence generation. |
| “Integration will cost more than the benefit.” | Test a narrow read/write or report interface, implementation time and avoided duplicate work before a broader integration. |
| “Our consultation economics cannot absorb another fee.” | Quantify time, completeness, capacity and risk-management value; validate a group-level price before choosing per-consult pricing. |
| “GPs will see this as fragmented care.” | Preserve patient choice, referral/escalation and timely clinical communication; include referral partners in evaluation. |

**Strongest commercial falsifier:** the approved clinical system already provides the assurance buyers value, leaving no material problem worth paying Makoha to solve. P1.2 validation must test this directly, not position around it.

## Evidence-based customer language

The following is public programme/practitioner language, not yet Makoha customer testimony:

- “Every consult moves us toward being recognised as an important part of primary care.” — [Queensland programme newsletter](https://www.health.qld.gov.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0025/1486105/Queensland-Community-Pharmacy-Pilot-Newsletter-17-October-2025.pdf)
- Queensland's AI alert emphasises that the practitioner remains responsible for appropriate, timely and accurate documentation.
- WA programme material repeatedly frames the service around timely, patient-centred care, defined inclusion/exclusion criteria, referrals and secure communication.

These phrases suggest that professional recognition, safe access, documentation and continuity matter. They must not be presented as endorsements of Makoha.

| ID | Assumption | Confidence | Test |
