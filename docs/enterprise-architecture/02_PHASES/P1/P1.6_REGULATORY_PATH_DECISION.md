## 3. Primary-source regulatory findings

1. Current TGA guidance states that AI-enabled CDSS will not meet the CDSS exemption criteria. It also requires an exempt recommendation to be transparent and not generated through proprietary analysis or AI. [TGA CDSS guidance](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/understanding-clinical-decision-support-system-software-regulation)
2. Australian classification follows intended purpose and the highest applicable rule across the device's functions. [TGA active-device classification guidance](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/classifying-active-medical-devices-australia-including-software-based-medical-devices)
3. Direct diagnosis/screening or treatment/intervention specification for a high-consequence condition can be Class III. Providing the corresponding information or recommendation only to a relevant health professional is generally Class IIb under Rules 4.5(2) or 4.7(2). [TGA software classification guidance](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/understanding-if-changes-software-based-medical-device-regulation-affect-you)
4. Software-based medical devices that are not excluded or exempt must be included in the ARTG before supply. Class IIa–III devices require manufacturer certification or acceptable comparable-overseas evidence. [TGA software regulation overview](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/understanding-how-we-regulate-software-based-medical-devices)
5. An unapproved software medical device used in a clinical trial remains regulated; the Australian trial sponsor must use an applicable CTN or CTA pathway. [TGA software medical-device FAQs](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/medical-devices/software-and-artificial-intelligence-ai/manufacturing/artificial-intelligence-ai-and-medical-device-software-regulation/software-based-medical-devices-faqs)
6. TGA identifies IEC 62304 and IEC 62366-1 as key software-device standards and ISO 14971 and ISO 13485 as relevant supporting standards. These are recognised best practice but are not, by themselves, a complete or mandatory compliance recipe. [TGA software standards](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/medical-devices/software-and-artificial-intelligence-ai/overview/standards-software-based-medical-devices)

## 4. Decision

### 4.1 Regulatory base case

Makoha will proceed on a **regulated medical-device-software / SaMD base case**, not an exempt-CDSS base case.

The shared platform will be engineered to a **Class III-capable assurance target**. Formal classification will be applied to each genuinely separable product boundary using its exact intended purpose. No public statement may claim “Class III”, “Class IIb”, TGA approval or ARTG inclusion until supported by the corresponding regulatory evidence.

### 4.2 Product family and provisional classification

| Product | Intended recipient/function | Provisional AU class | Decision state |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Makoha Triage** | Direct patient/user safety triage and disposition across presentations that may require urgent treatment | **Class III candidate** | Adopt as classification base case; counsel/TGA confirmation required |
| **Makoha Professional Assurance** | Information and management/intervention recommendations only to registered pharmacists or medical practitioners, who decide and sign | **Class IIb candidate** | Separate device boundary; confirmation required |
| **Makoha Record and Workflow Services** | Storage, retrieval, transfer, consent, audit and administrative workflow without therapeutic analysis | **Separate non-device/lower-risk assessment** | Must remain technically and contractually segregated |

If the functions cannot be separated in design, labelling, access, release control and intended purpose, the highest applicable classification governs the combined device.

## 5. Frozen intended-purpose statements — v0.1 for counsel and design control

### 5.1 Makoha Triage

> Makoha Triage is medical-device software intended to collect and analyse health information provided during a remote or digitally supported care interaction, identify presentations that may require urgent or emergency assessment, and provide the user with a disposition to seek emergency care, obtain prompt assessment by a relevant health professional, or proceed to a defined health-professional-led pathway. It is not intended to provide autonomous treatment or replace emergency services or a health professional. Incorrect or absent disposition may delay time-critical treatment.

**Initial claim constraints:**

- Australian use only.
- No autonomous prescribing or definitive treatment.
- Emergency escalation is non-overridable by the user or a lower-level model decision.
- “Proceed” means eligible to continue to professional assessment; it does not mean medically safe or free of serious illness.
- `[MISSING: initial age range and condition set]` — recommended first submission assumption: adults only, with paediatric use excluded until separately validated.

### 5.2 Makoha Professional Assurance

> Makoha Professional Assurance is medical-device software intended to provide registered pharmacists and medical practitioners with traceable information and recommendations that support history collection, red-flag identification, differential consideration, investigation, treatment/intervention selection, prescribing review, escalation and clinical documentation. The relevant health professional independently reviews the evidence, makes the clinical decision, and signs the record. The software is not intended to replace professional judgement or autonomously diagnose, prescribe or treat.

### 5.3 Makoha Record and Workflow Services

> Makoha Record and Workflow Services stores, retrieves, transfers and displays patient-authorised information, consent records, provenance, audit records and administrative forms. It does not analyse information to diagnose, screen, monitor, predict, recommend treatment or otherwise achieve a therapeutic purpose.

Any summarisation, prioritisation, red-flagging or therapeutic interpretation must occur inside a regulated product boundary, not be quietly added to the administrative service.

## 6. Mandatory architecture controls

1. Separate intended purposes, labels, instructions, user roles, APIs and release trains for each product.
2. Assign an immutable product/version identifier to every clinical output and gate record.
3. Keep direct patient triage logic inside the Class III-targeted boundary.
4. Keep professional recommendations inaccessible to patients before professional sign-off.
5. Preserve the scoring-store/presentation-store firewall and evidence provenance.
6. Treat every change to model, prompt, dataset, clinical content, intended purpose or output pathway as a regulated change-impact event.
7. Prohibit uncontrolled online learning in a released clinical version.
8. Require fail-closed behaviour on unavailable evidence, identity mismatch, stale data, dose uncertainty, unsupported scope or model failure.
9. Make emergency escalation monotonic and non-overridable.
10. Never label an outcome “safe”; use bounded dispositions and explicit uncertainty.

## 7. Enterprise QMS and evidence route

### Q0 — Regulatory foundation

**Outputs:** approved intended purposes, product-boundary diagram, Rules 4.5–4.8 matrix, manufacturer/sponsor determination, standards-applicability matrix and regulatory strategy.

**Exit:** signed classification memorandum and Management approval.

### Q1 — Quality-system foundation

**Outputs:** quality manual, document/record control, design and development control, risk management, supplier control, competence/training, software configuration/change control, nonconformance, CAPA, complaint/vigilance and management-review procedures.

**Exit:** controlled QMS effective date, trained owners and internal-audit plan.

### Q2 — Design-history reconstruction and forward controls

**Outputs:** user needs, system/software requirements, architecture, software safety classification, SOUP inventory, traceability matrix, risk-management file, usability file, cybersecurity file, verification plans and design-review records.

**Exit:** every released requirement and risk control traces to objective verification evidence; historical evidence is classified as valid, remediated or unusable.

### Q3 — Analytical and clinical validation

**Outputs:** analytical-validation protocol, locked models/data/content, representative test set, subgroup/error analysis, human-factors validation, clinical-evaluation plan/report and prospective-investigation plan where needed.

**Safety floor:** zero critical under-triage is a release criterion, not proof by itself. Sample sizes and confidence bounds must support the intended claims.

**Exit:** independent clinical and statistical sign-off on evidence adequacy.

### Q4 — Conformity assessment and ARTG

**Outputs:** Essential Principles checklist, technical documentation/design dossier, declaration of conformity, manufacturer certification/comparable-overseas evidence, labels/IFU, clinical evidence report and ARTG application.

For a Class III product, the selected route must cover the QMS and device design; Class III applications without a TGA conformity certificate are subject to application audit. [TGA Class III application principles](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/reclassifying-surgical-mesh-devices)

**Exit:** applicable certification and ARTG inclusion before supply.

### Q5 — Enterprise operations and post-market control

**Outputs:** production release authority, monitoring/alerting, complaint intake, adverse-event reporting, CAPA, periodic safety review, vulnerability response, field-action/recall procedure, model/data drift controls and post-market clinical follow-up.

**Exit:** simulated incident, recall and CAPA exercises passed; management review accepts residual risk.

## 9. Hard gates

The following are blocking only for the activity named:

| Activity | Gate |
|---|---|
| Synthetic development and documentation | May proceed under repository controls |
| QMS establishment and retrospective design-history work | May proceed now |
| De-identified retrospective research | Privacy, data-rights, ethics and regulatory determination |
| Prospective use of unapproved SaMD in a clinical investigation | HREC/site governance plus applicable CTN/CTA and sponsor controls |
| Clinical supply or patient-facing use | Applicable conformity evidence, ARTG inclusion and organisational release authorisation |
| Expanded intended purpose, model or clinical scope | Formal change impact, risk review, evidence update and classification reassessment |

This is why regulation is an enterprise workstream rather than organisational paralysis: much of the work proceeds now, while unsafe or unlawful supply remains blocked.

## 10. Strongest objection

The strongest objection is capital and execution load. Class III-capable design controls, clinical evidence, independent assessment and post-market operations can overwhelm a founder-led venture if pursued as an undifferentiated “certify everything” program.

Mitigation: one shared QMS, separable product boundaries, one narrow initial intended purpose, evidence reuse where legitimate, and staged design-control reconstruction. The company should not expand the first submission's age range, conditions or autonomous functions merely because the repository can technically support them.
