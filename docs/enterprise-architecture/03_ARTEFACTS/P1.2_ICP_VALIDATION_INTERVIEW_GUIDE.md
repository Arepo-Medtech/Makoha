# P1.2 ICP Validation Interview Guide

**Status:** READY — TARGETS AND INTERVIEWS REQUIRED  
**Purpose:** test the ICP and problem before product, pricing or pilot commitments  
**Safety boundary:** no clinical advice, no product supply, no identifiable patient data, and no claim that Makoha is approved or validated

## Sprint design

Conduct 12 structured interviews:

| Interviewee | Count | Required mix |
|---|---:|---|
| Pharmacy owner, operations lead or clinical-governance lead | 4 | At least two multi-site; include WA and Queensland where access permits |
| Trained/authorised pharmacist prescriber | 6 | Mix of experienced and newly trained; at least two regional |
| Patient/consumer representative with relevant pharmacy-care experience | 2 | Include access, privacy, cost, consent and continuity questions |

Do not count a combined owner/pharmacist interview twice. Record both role perspectives but count one interview.

### Pharmacy 777 allocation

- Seek one group-level buyer/clinical-governance interview.
- Seek two pharmacist-user interviews: preferably one Toowoomba/Queensland prescriber and one WA pharmacist preparing for or operating expanded scope.
- Use the remaining interviews for independent comparator evidence; Pharmacy 777 must not be allowed to become the only source validating the ICP.
- All discussions are non-exclusive and exploratory. No commitment, preferred-provider right or clinical deployment is implied.

## Opening script

“We are researching how expanded-scope pharmacy consultations are governed and documented. This is not a product demonstration or clinical consultation. We want to understand your current process, where it fails and what evidence would justify changing it. With your permission, I will take non-identifying notes. Please do not disclose patient-identifiable information.”

## Core questions for buyers

1. Walk me through how an expanded-scope service is introduced across your sites.
2. Who is accountable for clinical quality, records, incidents and referrals?
3. Where do you see the greatest variation between pharmacists or stores?
4. Tell me about the last incomplete record, escalation concern, complaint or audit finding. What happened next?
5. How do you measure record completeness, same-day finalisation, prescribing appropriateness and communication?
6. What does the current clinical system handle well? Where does it leave manual work or uncertainty?
7. What has this problem cost in time, capacity, delayed rollout, rework or risk?
8. Which three problems most constrain expansion? Force-rank them.
9. What evidence would you require before evaluating an additional assurance layer?
10. Who would approve the evaluation, integration, privacy assessment and budget?
11. How is technology for this service normally funded: site, user, consultation, programme or patient fee?
12. If a tightly governed evaluation met agreed measures, what concrete next step could you authorise?

## Core questions for pharmacist users

1. Reconstruct a recent eligible consultation without patient identifiers.
2. Which steps require the most judgement, checking or documentation?
3. Where are exclusions, red flags or referral decisions easiest to miss?
4. What must be completed in the clinical system, and what is duplicated elsewhere?
5. How often is the record not finalised during the consultation, and why?
6. What prompts, templates or decision support do you use now?
7. When has that support been unhelpful, wrong, distracting or too rigid?
8. Force-rank your top three workflow or safety problems.
9. What would make AI assistance unsafe, professionally unacceptable or deskilling?
10. What information must always remain visible and under your control?
11. What would save meaningful time without weakening the consultation?
12. What result would make you recommend—and what result would make you reject—a pilot?

## Core questions for patients/consumer representatives

1. What makes a pharmacy consultation feel safe and trustworthy?
2. What do you need to know about eligibility, price and alternatives before agreeing?
3. How important are privacy, a private room and knowing who can access the record?
4. How should the pharmacist explain the use of software or AI assistance?
5. What would make you refuse or withdraw consent?
6. How should the pharmacy handle uncertainty, escalation and communication with your usual GP?
7. Which matters most: speed, location, cost, privacy, explanation or continuity? Force-rank them.
8. What evidence would increase—or reduce—your trust?

## Evidence captured after every interview

- Role, state, site/group size and service maturity; no unnecessary personal data.
- Current workflow and clinical-system name.
- Verbatim problem language, with permission and attribution status.
- Top-three problem ranking.
- Frequency, consequence and current workaround.
- Existing-system overlap.
- Decision-maker, budget path and procurement constraints.
- Evaluation success/failure measure.
- Concrete next step, owner and date.
- Contradictory or disconfirming evidence.

## Pass, revise and kill thresholds

**PASS to design-partner offer:**

- At least 5 of 6 pharmacists place documentation completeness, exclusions/red flags, escalation/communication or related assurance in their top three problems.
- At least 2 of 4 buyers provide a written next step toward a governed evaluation.
- At least one buyer supplies a usable current-workflow or audit-baseline artefact.
- A non-duplicative role alongside the approved clinical system is demonstrated.
- No unresolved patient-safety, privacy or professional-accountability objection makes the concept unacceptable.

**REVISE:** pain is real but the buyer, workflow location, feature boundary or pricing unit differs materially from the provisional ICP.

**KILL OR RE-WEDGE:** the approved clinical system already solves the material problem; fewer than 3 of 6 pharmacists rank it in their top three; no buyer offers a next step; or value depends on autonomous clinical decisions or premature patient-facing use.
