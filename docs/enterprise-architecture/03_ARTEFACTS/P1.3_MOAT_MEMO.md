# Makoha Moat Memo — Permissioned Longitudinal Assurance

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 21 July 2026  
**Status:** PROVISIONAL THESIS — DATA RIGHTS AND OUTCOMES UNPROVEN

## Thesis in one sentence

Makoha can become defensible by turning permissioned expanded-scope pharmacy consultations into a uniquely traceable, clinically governed and regulator-ready assurance system—not by owning patient data or relying on model weights.

## Defensible asset stack

### 1. Structured assurance events

For each lawful, authorised workflow, Makoha can record a provenance-preserving sequence such as:

- presentation, scope and eligibility inputs;
- exclusions, red flags and uncertainty;
- source/protocol version and evidence shown;
- pharmacist decision, rationale, edit or override;
- escalation, referral and communication;
- documentation completion and timing; and
- permitted follow-up and outcome measures.

The useful asset is not raw volume. It is comparable, high-quality, labelled evidence with source, time, user, product version and decision provenance.

### 2. Safety and effectiveness evidence

A maintained hazard analysis, safety case, verification suite, human-factors evidence, clinical evaluation and post-market performance history are slow to create and difficult to imitate credibly. Each controlled deployment can improve the evidence package if the protocol, data rights and quality controls permit it.

### 3. Regulated operating capability

The Class III-capable QMS, design history, supplier/model controls, change-impact assessment, CAPA, audit, cybersecurity and post-market processes are a cumulative operating asset. Compliance alone is not a moat, but reliable execution and inspection-ready evidence raise the cost of imitation.

### 4. Workflow embedding

Reusable adapters into clinical information, identity, referral, audit and reporting workflows can reduce switching friction and deployment cost. This is defensible only if adapters are reusable and Makoha remains complementary rather than duplicative.

### 5. Cross-site learning with permission

De-identified and aggregated cross-site benchmarks could reveal where consultations fail, which workflow controls improve completeness, and how performance varies by setting. Makoha must not claim a network effect until contracts, privacy assessment, data quality and actual performance show that each additional authorised deployment improves the product or evidence base.

### 6. Pharmacist-to-doctor continuity network

If lawfully structured, reusable handover protocols, contracted doctor availability, measured response times and closed-loop outcomes can become an operating advantage. The defensible asset is reliable continuity across the pharmacist boundary—not referral volume. No network moat may be claimed until providers are contracted, service levels are met and clinical routing is shown to be independent of revenue.

## Rights and trust boundaries

The moat must be built on the following default principles:

1. Patients and pharmacy entities retain all rights they hold in identifiable health and business data; no ownership transfer is implied.
2. Makoha owns its background platform, schemas, controls and generalisable know-how, subject to contract and law.
3. Derived and aggregate performance measures are used only where expressly permitted and appropriately de-identified.
4. No customer or patient data is used for model training without specific, informed and documented authority.
5. Contracts define controller/processor roles, purposes, retention, deletion, portability, audit, sub-processors, incident handling and cross-site aggregation.
6. Site participation does not imply group authority, and group approval does not replace site, professional, ethics or patient authority.

## Asset-development sequence

| Horizon | Defensible asset expected | Evidence required |
|---|---|---|
| Entry | Intended-purpose discipline, workflow schema, safety case, audit/provenance and controlled deployment method | Verification, QMS records, counsel pathway, synthetic evidence |
| 1–3 partners | Reusable integrations, cross-site process benchmarks, controlled human-factors and safety evidence | Signed rights, comparable measures, independently reviewed results |
| Scale | Longitudinal effectiveness evidence and a licensable assurance layer across systems/jurisdictions | Regulatory approvals, production performance, lawful data coverage and repeatable economics |

## Moat metrics

- Percentage of events with complete provenance and lawful data rights.
- Number of reusable—not bespoke—workflow/integration modules.
- Cross-site comparability and missingness rates.
- Safety-control effectiveness and unresolved hazard/CAPA trends.
- Time and cost to deploy an additional site.
- Independently substantiated improvement in completeness, escalation, audit or efficiency.
- Renewal and expansion attributable to evidence value.
- Percentage of product improvements supported by authorised, traceable evidence.

## Failure conditions

The moat thesis fails if Makoha cannot obtain lawful derived-data rights; data are too inconsistent for comparison; customers prohibit all generalisable learning; integrations remain bespoke; existing systems close the gap; or the evidence does not improve safety, quality or economics.

## Current conclusion

The thesis is strategically credible but unproven. The first Pharmacy 777 discovery should test the data and authority architecture without collecting patient data. Any later evidence phase requires explicit data schedules, an approved protocol and applicable regulatory, ethics, privacy and site gates.
